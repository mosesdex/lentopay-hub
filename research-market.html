Nigerian Crypto Market & Regulatory Landscape — Research File

Project: Lentopay Exchange (Nigeria-first crypto exchange: buy/sell/swap, crypto bill payments, gift card trading, crypto savings/yield, "E-Fund" tokenized/unlisted-asset investing) Compiled: 8 July 2026 | Method: Web-sourced research; every factual claim carries a source. Preference given to 2024–2026 sources. Caveat: Figures from secondary reporting should be re-verified against primary documents (SEC circulars, CBN guidelines, gazetted Acts) before being used in legal filings or investor materials.

1. Market Size & Adoption

1.1 Adoption rankings and transaction volume

  • Nigeria ranked 6th globally on the Chainalysis 2025 Global Crypto Adoption Index (down from 2nd in prior years), while ranking 2nd globally on grassroots adoption specifically — the drop is attributed partly to regulatory tightening closing informal channels and temporarily lowering measured volume, not collapsing demand. (Source: Chainalysis / Technext, Sep 2025, https://technext24.com/2025/09/04/crypto-nigeria-slips-india-and-u-s-lead/ and https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/2025-global-crypto-adoption-index/)
  • Nigeria received $92.1 billion in on-chain value between July 2024 and June 2025 — the largest in Sub-Saharan Africa and nearly 3x South Africa. The whole SSA region received $205B+, up ~52% year-on-year, making it the third-fastest-growing crypto region globally. (Source: Chainalysis 2025 Geography of Cryptocurrency Report, Oct 2025, https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/subsaharan-africa-crypto-adoption-2025/)
  • An earlier IMF-cited figure: about $59 billion in crypto-asset inflows between July 2023 and June 2024. (Source: Leadership.ng citing IMF, 2025, https://leadership.ng/nigerians-turning-to-stablecoins-for-remittances-amid-59bn-crypto-inflows-imf/)
  • Nigeria ranked 3rd globally for DeFi adoption in the 2025 Chainalysis index. (Source: Chainalysis, Oct 2025, https://www.chainalysis.com/reports/2025-geo-crypto-report/)
  • A March 2025 volume surge was driven largely by centralized-exchange activity after a sudden naira devaluation — the defining behavioral signature of the Nigerian market: devaluation → stablecoin flight. (Source: Chainalysis, Oct 2025, https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/subsaharan-africa-crypto-adoption-2025/)

1.2 Stablecoin dominance

  • Stablecoins account for ~43% of Sub-Saharan Africa's total crypto transaction volume; Nigeria captures roughly 40% of all stablecoin inflows in the region — the highest concentration in SSA. Volume is dominated by USDT and USDC. (Source: Chainalysis via Mariblock, Oct 2025, https://www.mariblock.com/sub-saharan-africa-now-the-third-fastest-growing-crypto-region-chainalysis/)
  • Nigeria processed nearly $22 billion in stablecoin transactions between July 2023 and June 2024, leading Africa. (Source: Plasma research page, 2025, https://www.plasma.org/learn/nigeria-stablecoins)

1.3 User base and demographics

  • Estimated ~22 million Nigerian crypto users (~10.3% of population) as of 2025. (Source: Mular blog, 2025, https://mular.co/blog/nigeria-crypto-ownership-2025/)
  • Surveyed user base skews very young: ~81.5% of crypto users aged 18–34 (41.9% aged 18–24; 39.6% aged 25–34); one KPMG-cited survey found 35% of Nigerian adults have invested in crypto with 52% of them under 30; gender split reported approaching parity (≈50% women among investors in one 2025 survey). (Source: Springer Nature study & KPMG Nigeria "Crypto Risk and Opportunities" paper, Mar 2025, https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s44257-025-00045-2 and https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmg/ng/pdf/2025/03/Crypto%20Risk%20and%20Opportunities_A_New%20Banking%20Paradigm%20March%2010%20update.pdf)
  • Most-used platforms in surveys: Binance (82.4%), Trust Wallet (65.5%), P2P channels (50.4%) — a mobile-first, self-custody-heavy market. (Source: Springer Nature, 2025, https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s44257-025-00045-2)
  • Nigeria's median age is ~18, one of the youngest populations globally. (Source: DataReportal Digital 2025: Nigeria, Feb 2025, https://datareportal.com/reports/digital-2025-nigeria)

1.4 Connectivity — the addressable ceiling

  • ~107 million internet users at the start of 2025 (~45.4% penetration). (Source: DataReportal, Feb 2025, https://datareportal.com/reports/digital-2025-nigeria)
  • Active telephone subscriptions reached 179.64 million by December 2025; internet subscriptions 112.67 million; broadband penetration ~51.97% (50.58% in Nov 2025) — still short of the 70% national target. (Source: The Guardian Nigeria / Tekedia / TechCabal, Dec 2025, https://guardian.ng/news/nigerias-active-telephone-users-hit-179-6m-as-data-consumption-surges/ and https://techcabal.com/2025/12/24/nigeria-50-percent-internet-penetration-broadband-target/)
  • Headwind: smartphone sales fell ~7% in H1 2025; an entry-level device costs more than three months' minimum wage — device affordability constrains the bottom of the market. (Source: Dabafinance, 2025, https://www.dabafinance.com/en/news/nigeria-smartphone-costs-internet-subscription-decline-telco)

1.5 Macro drivers: naira devaluation & remittances

  • The naira collapsed from ~₦460/$ (mid-June 2023) to ~₦1,600/$ (Jan 2024) after the CBN floated the currency in June 2023; it lost 51.5% of value in 2023 and closed 2024 at ₦1,535/$ (−40.9% for 2024); inflation ended 2024 at ~35%. This is the single biggest driver of stablecoin adoption. (Source: Nairametrics, Dec 31 2024, https://nairametrics.com/2024/12/31/exchange-rate-ends-2024-at-n1535-1-marking-a-40-9-depreciation/; Chatham House, Mar 2025, https://www.chathamhouse.org/2025/03/nigerias-economy-needs-naira-stay-competitive; Intelpoint, 2025, https://intelpoint.co/insights/nigerias-naira-depreciated-by-129-in-2024-hitting-an-average-of-1479-ngn-per-us/)
  • Nigeria receives ~$20 billion/year in remittances; traditional channels cost 5–12% in fees plus 3–5% FX haircut with 3–7 day settlement, vs. stablecoin transfers settling in minutes for under $1 on chains like Tron/Solana. Average traditional remittance fee ~8.45% (Q3 2024). The IMF has flagged rising stablecoin use for remittances. (Source: Plasma, 2025, https://www.plasma.org/learn/nigeria-stablecoins; Leadership.ng citing IMF, 2025, https://leadership.ng/nigerians-turning-to-stablecoins-for-remittances-amid-59bn-crypto-inflows-imf/)

1.6 P2P dynamics

  • After CBN/NSA pressure, Binance delisted the naira from its P2P platform on 1 March 2024 and halted all naira deposits/withdrawals by 8 March 2024; the CBN claimed >$26B flowed through Binance Nigeria in 2023 from unverifiable sources and alleged P2P was used to manipulate the naira rate. (Source: Businessday / Azasend, Mar 2024, https://businessday.ng/news/article/binance-bails-out-of-naira-operations-after-clampdown/ and https://azasend.com/news/binance-p2p-ban-in-nigeria-reasons-timeline-and-a-safer-alternative-for-crypto-traders/)
  • The SEC announced intent to delist the naira from P2P platforms in 2024; users migrated to Bybit, KuCoin, and informal Telegram/WhatsApp channels — while licensed local exchanges (Busha, Quidax) offer direct regulated naira trading. (Source: Cointelegraph, May 2024, https://cointelegraph.com/news/nigeria-ban-crypto-p2p-exchange; Nairametrics, Nov 2024, https://nairametrics.com/2024/11/05/experts-weigh-in-on-the-dangers-of-unregulated-p2p-trading-in-nigeria/)
  • Strategic read: regulated on/off-ramps are now the compliant substitute for the P2P gray market — this is Lentopay's core opportunity and the SEC's stated preference.

2.2 Investments and Securities Act (ISA) 2025

  • Signed by President Tinubu on March 25, 2025, repealing the ISA 2007. It explicitly recognizes digital/virtual assets as securities, defining a digital asset as "a digital token that represents assets such as a debt or equity claim on the issuer," capturing cryptocurrencies, stablecoins, security tokens, and tokenized real-world assets. (Source: TechCabal, May 9 2025, https://techcabal.com/2025/05/09/investments-and-securities-act-nigeria-2025/; Andersen Nigeria, 2025, https://ng.andersen.com/the-investment-and-securities-act-2025-a-new-era-for-digital-assets-and-financial-disclosure/; UUBO briefing, May 2025, https://uubo.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/WHAT-THE-INVESTMENT-AND-SECURITIES-ACT-2025-MEANS-FOR-DIGITAL-ASSETS-IN-NIGERIA.pdf)
  • Digital asset issuers, exchanges, wallet providers, custodians and advisers all fall under SEC oversight; licensing/registration with fit-and-proper criteria is mandatory. (Source: Pavestones Legal, 2025, https://pavestoneslegal.com/impact-of-the-investment-securities-act-2025-on-ownership-and-use-of-virtual-assets-in-nigeria/)
  • ISA 2025 criminalizes Ponzi schemes: up to 10 years imprisonment and ₦40 million fine for operators/promoters. (Source: Punch, Apr 2025, https://punchng.com/ponzi-scams-cbex-crash-highlights-need-for-public-awareness-investment-regulation/)

2.3 SEC Digital Assets Rules & VASP licensing

  • Base framework: SEC "New Rules on Issuance, Offering Platforms and Custody of Digital Assets" (May 2022) with license classes: VASP, Digital Assets Exchange (DAX), Digital Assets Offering Platform (DAOP), Digital Assets Custodian (DAC). (Source: SEC Nigeria rules PDF, 2022, https://sec.gov.ng/documents/8/Rules-on-Issuance-Offering-and-Custody-of-Digital-Assets.pdf)
  • Amendments were exposed Sept 27, 2024 and the amended rules took effect June 30, 2025, superseding the earlier "Statement on Digital Assets Classification and Treatment." (Source: Templars, Jan 2025, https://www.templars-law.com/app/uploads/2025/01/SEC-Further-Tightens-Rules-on-Digital-Assets-with-New-Amendments.pdf; Olaniwun Ajayi, 2025, https://www.olaniwunajayi.net/blog/understanding-the-nigerian-digital-and-virtual-assets-landscape-key-changes/)
  • Capital & fees (as of licensing under the 2022 rules): minimum paid-up capital ₦500,000,000 (bank balances, fixed assets or quoted securities), fidelity bond ≥25% of paid-up capital, registration fee ₦30,000,000 plus application/processing/sponsored-individual fees. (Source: Goldsmiths LLP, 2025, https://www.goldsmithsllp.com/how-virtual-assets-service-providers-can-obtain-licenses-in-nigeria/; Mandel Solicitors, 2025, https://mandelsolicitors.com/how-to-register-and-obtain-license-as-a-virtual-assets-service-provider-vasp-in-nigeria/)
  • Capital raised in 2026: SEC Circular No. 26-1 (dated January 16, 2026) raises minimum capital for Digital Assets Exchanges and Custodians to ₦2 billion (~$1.4M), up from ₦500M (and above the ₦1B floated in 2024 consultations); tokenization platforms ₦1B. Compliance deadline June 30, 2027; non-compliance risks suspension/withdrawal of registration. Industry reaction split — feared to squeeze early-stage local firms. (Source: TechCabal, Jan 16 2026, https://techcabal.com/2026/01/16/sec-2-billion-minimum-capital-for-exchanges/; Nairametrics, Jan 16 2026, http://nairametrics.com/2026/01/16/sec-raises-capital-requirement-for-crypto-exchanges-in-nigeria-to-n2-billion/; Businessday, 2026, https://businessday.ng/technology/article/secs-n2bn-crypto-capital-rule-splits-industry-as-operators-weigh-stability-against-survival/)

2.4 ARIP — the actual licensing path

  • ARIP launched by SEC circular of June 21, 2024 to onboard VASPs that pre-dated the 2022 rules; requires: incorporation in Nigeria with a physical Nigerian office, CEO/MD resident in Nigeria, ₦2,000,000 non-refundable processing fee, evidence of shareholders' funds + fidelity bond ≥25%, SEC Forms 2/2D for at least four principal officers, filing via a registered solicitor, and a "no objection" letter from any sectoral regulator. (Source: SEC Nigeria ARIP checklist, 2024, https://sec.gov.ng/about/resources/checklists/accelerated-regulatory-incubation-program-arip-checklist-for-vasp-onboarding/; UUBO, Jul 2024, https://uubo.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/An-Overview-of-the-Secs-Framework-on-Accelerated-Regulatory-Incubation-Programme-for-the-onboarding-of-the-VASPS-and-DIPS.pdf; Techpoint, Jul 4 2024, https://techpoint.africa/2024/07/04/sec-nigerian-offices-virtual-asset-companies/)
  • Approval-in-Principle (AIP) is granted for up to 12 months; firms then "regularize" into full registration after meeting all requirements. (Source: Goldsmiths LLP / Mondaq, 2025, https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/fin-tech/1695342/how-virtual-assets-service-providers-vasps-can-obtain-licenses-in-nigeria)
  • Who has licenses: Busha and Quidax received the first provisional licenses on August 29, 2024; five others (Trovotech, Wrapped CBDC Ltd, HousingExchange.NG, Dream City Capital, Blockvault Custodian) were admitted to the incubation sandbox. (Source: TechCabal, Aug 29 2024, https://techcabal.com/2024/08/29/sec-crypto-licences/; Punch, Aug 2024, https://punchng.com/sec-grants-busha-quidax-provisional-crypto-licences/)
  • July 3, 2026: SEC granted AIP to seven more firms — Bitbarter, Luno Fintech Nigeria, GetEquity, KoinKoin, Wrapped CBDC, Trovotech, Blockvault Custodian — bringing total AIP holders to at least nine; KuCoin Nigeria and GIGX Technologies were also recently admitted to ARIP. AIP is explicitly not a final operating licence. (Source: Nairametrics, Jul 3 2026, http://nairametrics.com/2026/07/03/breaking-sec-grants-approval-in-principle-to-7-crypto-firms-under-regulatory-sandbox/; Technext, Jul 3 2026, https://technext24.com/2026/07/03/sec-clears-luno-koinkoin-and-5-others/)
  • SEC pace: DG Agama has said the Commission is in "no rush" and needs deep due diligence per applicant; but at a Dec 2024 workshop it committed to moving faster in 2025 — borne out by the 2026 cohort. (Source: CoinGeek, 2024, https://coingeek.com/nigeria-sec-were-in-no-rush-to-issue-new-licenses/; TechCabal, Feb 6 2025, https://techcabal.com/2025/02/06/sec-to-issue-crypto-licences-faster/)
  • Stakeholders have publicly criticized VASP licensing fees/capital as "economically unrealistic" for local startups. (Source: CoinGeek, 2024, https://coingeek.com/nigeria-vasp-licensing-fees-are-economically-unrealistic-stakeholders-say/)

2.5 CBN position — banking rails for VASPs

  • February 5, 2021: CBN barred banks and other financial institutions from servicing crypto businesses, citing ML/TF risk. (Source: FinTech Futures, Feb 2021, https://www.fintechfutures.com/2021/02/nigerias-central-bank-issues-crypto-shutdown-throwing-fintechs-into-disarray/)
  • December 22, 2023: CBN "Guidelines on Operations of Bank Accounts for Virtual Asset Service Providers" (ref FPR/DIR/PUB/CIR/002/003) lifted the ban — banks may open designated accounts for SEC-licensed VASPs only, subject to enhanced due diligence; banks remain barred from holding/trading crypto on their own account. Practical effect: no SEC license → no Nigerian bank account for a crypto business. (Source: CBN guidelines PDF, Dec 2023, https://www.cbn.gov.ng/out/2024/fprd/guidelines%20on%20operations%20of%20bank%20accounts%20for%20virtual%20asset%20providers.pdf; Nairametrics, Dec 22 2023, https://nairametrics.com/2023/12/22/cbn-releases-guidelines-for-regulating-virtual-assets-in-nigeria/; PwC note, 2024, https://www.pwc.com/ng/en/assets/pdf/cbn-guidelines-on-operations-of-bank-accounts-for-virtual-assets-service-providers.pdf)
  • March 31, 2026: CBN launched a pilot supervisory/compliance programme covering virtual-asset-related activity at major PSPs including Flutterwave and Paystack — signaling CBN is re-entering crypto oversight alongside the SEC. (Source: Technext, Apr 1 2026, https://technext24.com/2026/04/01/flutterwave-paystack-6-cbn-vasps/; Innovation Village, 2026, https://innovation-village.com/cbn-introduces-new-crypto-compliance-programme-for-flutterwave-paystack-and-other-vasps/)

2.6 Taxation (effective January 1, 2026)

  • The Nigeria Tax Act 2025 / Nigeria Tax Administration Act (NTAA) 2025 came into full effect January 1, 2026, creating a comprehensive digital-asset tax regime. (Source: Forbes, Feb 23 2026, https://www.forbes.com/sites/digital-assets/2026/02/23/what-nigerias-2026-tax-laws-mean-for-bitcoin-holders-and-vasps/; Technext, Oct 5 2025, https://technext24.com/2025/10/05/how-nigeria-plans-to-tax-crypto-2026/)
  • Treatment reported: gains on disposal of digital assets are chargeable gains; a base 10% CGT-style rate is referenced for disposals, but under the new law crypto profits can be taxed as income at progressive personal rates up to 25% for individuals (and CIT for companies), overriding the flat 10% digital-asset CGT introduced by the Finance Act 2022. Small gains under ₦10M may qualify for exemption/reduced rates. Airdrops and staking rewards are taxable income at fair market value on receipt. (Source: Mariblock, 2025, https://www.mariblock.com/nigeria-set-to-tax-individual-crypto-transactions-and-exchanges-in-2026/; Busha blog, 2025, https://busha.io/blog/how-nigerias-tax-law-affects-your-crypto; Apex Network blog, 2026, https://blog.apexnetwork.co/crypto-taxation-in-nigeria-2026-what-every-digital-asset-trader-needs-to-know/) Note: rate reporting varies across sources (a "15% crypto gains tax" debate also appears in some outlets) — obtain the gazetted Act text via Nigerian tax counsel before building tax logic. (Source: Cryptometer, 2026, https://www.cryptometer.io/news/nigerias-15-crypto-gains-tax-sparks-debate-over-regulatory-clarity/)
  • VASP obligation directly relevant to Lentopay: exchanges must file monthly reports to FIRS detailing user identities, transaction details, dates, asset types and naira values; penalties for non-filing start at ₦100,000 plus monthly charges and interest, with imprisonment possible for evasion. (Source: Technext, Oct 5 2025, https://technext24.com/2025/10/05/how-nigeria-plans-to-tax-crypto-2026/; Lawyard, 2025, https://www.lawyard.org/lawyard-spotlight/nigerias-cryptocurrency-tax-revolution/)

2.7 Data protection — NDPA 2023

  • The Nigeria Data Protection Act 2023 (effective June 12, 2023) replaced the NDPR 2019 and created the Nigeria Data Protection Commission (NDPC). (Source: Future of Privacy Forum, 2023, https://fpf.org/blog/nigerias-new-data-protection-act-explained/)
  • A crypto exchange will qualify as a "Data Controller of Major Importance" (processes >200 data subjects in 6 months / handles fiduciary financial data) — triggering mandatory NDPC registration, a DPO, DPIAs for high-risk processing, written DPAs with processors (KYC vendors!), and 72-hour breach notification to NDPC. (Source: SecurePrivacy, 2025, https://secureprivacy.ai/blog/nigeria-data-protection-law; CookieYes NDPA guide, 2025, https://www.cookieyes.com/blog/nigeria-data-protection-act-ndpa/)
  • Penalties: for controllers of major importance, up to ₦10M or 2% of annual gross revenue (whichever is higher). Cross-border transfers (e.g., to a foreign cloud/custody provider) require adequacy, appropriate safeguards (SCCs), or explicit consent. (Source: ICLG Nigeria Data Protection 2025–26, https://iclg.com/practice-areas/data-protection-laws-and-regulations/nigeria/)

2.8 AML/CFT & KYC

  • Core statutes: Money Laundering (Prevention and Prohibition) Act 2022 and Terrorism (Prevention and Prohibition) Act 2022, aligned with FATF; VASPs are treated as financial institutions with full AML program, KYC, transaction monitoring, sanctions/wallet screening (KYT), and STR obligations. (Source: ANQA Compliance, 2025, https://www.anqacompliance.com/nigeria-aml-giaba/; Sumsub West Africa spotlight, 2025, https://sumsub.com/media/spotlight/crypto-compliance-in-west-africa/)
  • Reporting timelines: CTRs within 24 hours; STRs within 7 days to the NFIU; tipping-off is a criminal offence. SCUML (under EFCC) supervises DNFBPs — failure to register where required is a criminal offence under the MLPPA 2022. (Source: ANQA Compliance, 2025, https://www.anqacompliance.com/nigeria-aml-giaba/; Global Law Experts, 2024, https://globallawexperts.com/a-guide-on-anti-money-laundering-counter-terrorism-finance-compliance-for-designated-non-financial-businesses-professions-in-nigeria/)
  • Travel Rule: Nigeria has signaled intent to adopt FATF thresholds but no gazetted instrument fixes the threshold yet; FATF expects implementation by 2030 — build travel-rule-capable messaging (e.g., via custody/compliance vendors) now. (Source: Sumsub, 2025, https://sumsub.com/media/spotlight/crypto-compliance-in-west-africa/)
  • Tiered KYC norms (CBN): Tier 1 — BVN or NIN, ₦30,000 daily limit (mandatory BVN/NIN linkage for all Tier-1 accounts/wallets since December 1, 2023, per CBN circular); Tier 2 — BVN + NIN + verified ID (₦500,000 daily); Tier 3 — full KYC incl. proof of address, no limits. Real-time automated BVN/NIN lookups via NIBSS/NIMC are the minimum standard. (Source: Youverify, 2026, https://youverify.co/en/blogs/cbn-kyc-aml-requirements-2026; PayCliq, 2024, https://blog.paycliq.africa/cbns-latest-bvn-and-nin-requirements-for-tier-1-accounts/)

2.9 The Binance–Nigeria dispute — implications

  • Feb 2024: executives Tigran Gambaryan and Nadeem Anjarwalla detained (Anjarwalla escaped custody); Gambaryan held ~7 months on ML/tax charges, released October 23, 2024 after charges dropped amid serious health concerns and diplomatic pressure. (Source: Blockhead, Feb 2025, https://www.blockhead.co/2025/02/20/nigeria-sues-binance-takes-another-swing-at-former-exec-tigran-gambaryan/)
  • Feb 2025: FIRS sued Binance for $81.5B — $79.5B "economic losses" for alleged naira destabilization + $2B back taxes with 26.75% interest, on a "significant economic presence" theory; tax evasion case adjourned to Apr 30, 2025; multiple related suits persist. (Source: CoinDesk, Feb 20 2025, https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2025/02/20/nigeria-sues-binance-for-usd81-5-billion-in-economic-losses-and-back-taxes; DL News, 2025, https://www.dlnews.com/articles/regulation/binance-faces-legal-ordeal-in-nigeria-amid-81b-usd-fine/)
  • Implications for Lentopay: (1) operating without local incorporation/licensing is treated as a national-security-level offense; (2) "significant economic presence" creates tax exposure even for offshore entities serving Nigerians; (3) executives are personally at risk; (4) the state will link FX instability to crypto platforms — expect data demands (Nigeria demanded Binance's top-100 user data in Mar 2024). Local incorporation, licensing and proactive regulator relations are non-negotiable. (Source: PYMNTS, 2024, https://www.pymnts.com/cryptocurrency/2024/nigeria-moves-ban-p2p-naira-crypto-trading/)

2.10 Feature-by-feature regulatory analysis (PRD mapping)

(a) Crypto savings / yield

  • Under ISA 2025, a yield-bearing crypto product is almost certainly an investment contract/security requiring SEC registration of both product and platform. Post-June-2025 rules, SEC-registered platforms reportedly offer regulated stablecoin yield (8–14% APY marketed) subject to proof-of-reserves audits, custody insurance, and Nigerian-licensed custodians; industry practice includes quarterly PoR and insurance filings with the SEC. (Source: CorporateFinance.ng, 2025, https://corporatefinance.ng/nigeria-crypto-boom-earn-dollar-stablecoin-yields/; Chambers Blockchain 2025 Nigeria, https://practiceguides.chambers.com/practice-guides/blockchain-2025/nigeria/trends-and-developments)
  • Risk: anything resembling "guaranteed returns" collides with post-CBEX Ponzi enforcement (10 yrs / ₦40M under ISA 2025). Marketing language must avoid fixed-return promises; disclose yield source. (Source: Punch, Apr 2025, https://punchng.com/ponzi-scams-cbex-crash-highlights-need-for-public-awareness-investment-regulation/)
  • Recommendation: phase 2+ feature; launch only after full VASP registration and specific SEC product approval, with a licensed Digital Assets Custodian holding assets.

(b) "E-Fund" unlisted/tokenized asset investing

  • Tokenized real-world assets and debt/equity claims are squarely "digital assets = securities" under ISA 2025; offering them requires a Digital Assets Offering Platform (DAOP) registration and per-offer SEC clearance. (Source: UUBO ISA-2025 briefing, May 2025, https://uubo.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/WHAT-THE-INVESTMENT-AND-SECURITIES-ACT-2025-MEANS-FOR-DIGITAL-ASSETS-IN-NIGERIA.pdf)
  • If structured as crowdfunding of MSME instruments: SEC Crowdfunding Rules (Jan 21, 2021) apply — Crowdfunding Intermediary license (min share capital ₦100M), mandatory nominee/custodial structure, raise caps per 12 months (₦100M medium / ₦70M small / ₦50M micro enterprises), 60+30-day offer windows, funds released only at minimum threshold. ISA 2025 adds mandatory registration of all crowdfunded securities and pre-clearance of every offer. (Source: Aluko & Oyebode, 2021, https://www.aluko-oyebode.com/insights/the-sec-approved-rules-on-crowdfunding/; Financial Nigeria, 2025, https://www.financialnigeria.com/how-the-isa-2025-reshapes-nigeria-s-crowdfunding-regulation-feature-597.html)
  • Tokenization platforms face a ₦1B minimum capital under SEC Circular 26-1 (Jan 2026). GetEquity's admission to ARIP (Jul 2026) shows the SEC will sandbox this model — a precedent for E-Fund. (Source: TechCabal, Jan 16 2026, https://techcabal.com/2026/01/16/sec-2-billion-minimum-capital-for-exchanges/; Technext, Jul 3 2026, https://technext24.com/2026/07/03/sec-clears-luno-koinkoin-and-5-others/)
  • Recommendation: highest regulatory burden of all PRD features — gate to last phase, or partner with an already-licensed DAOP/crowdfunding intermediary.

(c) Gift card trading

  • No dedicated regulator: gift card trading itself is not an SEC "security" activity, but paying out in naira/crypto puts it inside VASP/AML perimeter. Market is large: ~$2B annually, an estimated 40–45M cards traded per year. (Source: WeeTracker, Sep 9 2025, https://weetracker.com/2025/09/09/inside-nigerias-usd-2-b-gift-card-economy-from-liquidity-fix-to-fintech-feature/)
  • Major AML red flag: law enforcement regularly cites iTunes/Steam/Amazon gift cards as proceeds channels for romance scams, sextortion and "Yahoo Yahoo" fraud — incumbents (Cardtonic, Prestmit, Nosh) now require BVN + government ID + selfie before any trade. Lentopay must match or exceed this. (Source: WeeTracker, Sep 2025, https://weetracker.com/2025/09/09/inside-nigerias-usd-2-b-gift-card-economy-from-liquidity-fix-to-fintech-feature/)
  • Operational fraud vectors: stolen/used card codes, fake payment receipts/bank alerts, social-media DM trading scams, rate-bait switch. Manual + automated card validation and delayed settlement windows are standard mitigations. (Source: Owodex best-practices, 2025, https://www.owodex.com/blog/secure-gift-card-trading-in-nigeria-15-best-practices-to-avoid-scams-in-2025/; Astro Africa, Jun 2025, https://astroafrica.site/2025/06/11/how-to-avoid-fake-gift-card-buyers-in-nigeria-2025-guide/)

(d) Bill payments

  • Direct provision of payment processing/aggregation requires a CBN PSSP license: refundable ₦100M capital deposit (lump sum), ₦100,000 application fee, ₦1M licence fee on approval; approval-in-principle valid 6 months. A PSSP cannot hold customer funds or issue wallets — that needs higher-tier licenses (super agent/PSP/MMO). (Source: Goldsmiths LLP, 2024, https://www.goldsmithsllp.com/how-to-obtain-a-payment-solution-service-providers-licence-in-nigeria/; Legal500 practice note, 2024, https://www.legal500.com/developments/thought-leadership/how-to-obtain-a-payment-solution-service-providers-licence-in-nigeria-2/)
  • Practical path: integrate a licensed aggregator (VTpass, Quickteller/Interswitch — 7,000+ billers, Baxi — 248 billers/460k agents, Monnify bills API) rather than licensing directly; the crypto leg (user pays in crypto, aggregator settles in naira) stays inside Lentopay's VASP perimeter. (Source: Interswitch docs, https://docs.interswitchgroup.com/docs/value-added-services-overview; Onafriq insights, https://onafriq.com/insights/article/selecting-the-right-bill-payment-api-in-nigeria; Monnify, https://monnify.com/products/bills-payment)

(e) Fiat on/off-ramp

  • Requires: SEC VASP license (prerequisite for bank accounts per CBN Dec 2023 guidelines) + banking partner willing to open designated VASP accounts + naira collection/payout rails. (Source: CBN guidelines, Dec 2023, https://www.cbn.gov.ng/out/2024/fprd/guidelines%20on%20operations%20of%20bank%20accounts%20for%20virtual%20asset%20providers.pdf)
  • Warning: mainstream PSPs still contractually exclude crypto. Paystack's ineligible-business list bars "non-fiat currency and cryptocurrency assets… sales of currency without BDC licence, cryptocurrency operators"; Flutterwave prohibits "digital wallet, cryptocurrency, or prepaid companies." Card-acquiring for a crypto app is therefore hard; bank-transfer virtual accounts (Providus, 9PSB, Monnify) are the realistic rail, negotiated bilaterally with the SEC license in hand. The CBN's Mar 2026 pilot bringing Flutterwave/Paystack into VASP-related supervision may loosen this over time. (Source: Paystack support, https://support.paystack.com/en/articles/2127042; Flutterwave help center, https://flutterwave.com/ng/support/my-account/businesses-and-services-prohibited-by-flutterwave; Technext, Apr 1 2026, https://technext24.com/2026/04/01/flutterwave-paystack-6-cbn-vasps/)

3. Risks & Failures to Learn From

3.1 Patricia hack & token debacle

  • Patricia (crypto + gift card exchange) disclosed in May 2023 a breach costing ~$2M in customer assets (reporting later suggested the underlying compromise dated to January 2022 — a disclosure-delay scandal in itself). (Source: Techpoint Africa, 2023, https://techpoint.africa/insight/how-patricia-plans-to-pay-customers-back/; CoinGeek, 2023, https://coingeek.com/nigeria-patricia-exchange-raises-exit-scam-fears-after-converting-user-funds-to-native-token/)
  • Patricia unilaterally converted customer balances into a non-on-chain IOU "Patricia Token (PTK)" (Oct 2023), pledging 1 PTK = 1 USDT, and later asked customers to convert debt to shares — triggering exit-scam accusations; repayments proceeded gradually through 2024. A politician was arrested for allegedly stealing ₦200M+ from Patricia's wallet. (Source: TechCabal, Aug 28 2023, https://techcabal.com/2023/08/28/patricias-ambitious-plan-to-repay-customers-has-one-important-caveat/; Cointelegraph, 2023, https://cointelegraph.com/news/nigeria-patricia-technologies-relieved-as-arrest-made-in-crypto-wallet-heist; Condia, 2024, https://thecondia.com/patricia-debt-repayment-method/)
  • Lessons: segregate customer assets with a licensed custodian; disclose breaches immediately (NDPA now mandates 72h); never socialize losses via forced token conversion; hot-wallet insurance and withdrawal-delay circuit breakers.

3.2 FTX contagion (2022)

  • FTX had 100,000+ African customers pre-collapse; Nigerian web3 startup Nestcoin held ~$4M of operational cash/stablecoins on FTX and laid off ~30 staff; retail Nigerians lost sums from thousands to millions of dollars. Lasting damage to consumer trust in centralized custody. (Source: TechCrunch, Nov 21 2022, https://techcrunch.com/2022/11/21/ftx-processed-billions-monthly-in-africa-before-going-bust/; TechCrunch, Nov 14 2022, https://techcrunch.com/2022/11/14/african-web3-startup-nestcoin-declares-it-held-its-assests-in-ftx-lays-off-employees/)
  • Lesson: proof-of-reserves from day one is a trust differentiator in Nigeria specifically.

3.3 CBEX and the Ponzi trust deficit

  • CBEX (fake "China Beijing Equity Exchange") operated from July 2024, promising 100% ROI in 30 days; collapsed April 10–15, 2025; 200,000+ Nigerians affected. Loss estimates range wildly: media claimed up to ₦1.3 trillion (EFCC partially recovered funds), while on-chain analysis (Techpoint) estimated only ~$6.1M of tracked deposits — either way the reputational shock was enormous. (Source: TechCabal, Apr 15 2025, https://techcabal.com/2025/04/15/cbex-ponzi-collapse-nigeria/; Nairametrics, May 26 2025, https://nairametrics.com/2025/05/26/efcc-has-recovered-part-of-n1-3-trillion-lost-to-cbex-ola-olukoyede-2/; Techpoint, 2025, https://techpoint.africa/insight/nigerians-loss-to-cbex/; Elliptic investigation, 2025, https://www.elliptic.co/blog/investigating-cbex)
  • SEC reports Nigerians lost ₦316 billion to Ponzi schemes (cumulative reporting, 2025). SEC issued formal Ponzi warnings on April 14, 2025; ISA 2025 now criminalizes operation/promotion (10 yrs, ₦40M). (Source: Pulse.ng, Nov 2025, https://www.pulse.ng/story/worst-ponzi-schemes-in-nigeria-2025110106084446283; Punch, 2025, https://punchng.com/cbex-crash-all-you-need-to-know-about-the-ponzi-scheme-that-duped-nigerians/)
  • Lesson: the word "investment" + "returns" is radioactive in 2026 Nigeria. Lentopay's savings/E-Fund features must lead with licensing, custody transparency and realistic yields — and expect SEC marketing scrutiny.

3.4 Fraud environment (fiat + gift cards)

  • NIBSS: digital payment fraud losses fell 51% to ₦25.85B in 2025 (from ₦52.26B in 2024); social engineering is the #1 technique; SIM-swap, account compromise, phishing fastest-growing; Lagos = 63% of cases. CBN now directs NIBSS to debit settlement accounts of institutions that receive fraud proceeds without due diligence — receiving-institution liability applies to any fintech running naira wallets/virtual accounts. (Source: NIBSS, 2026, https://nibss-plc.com.ng/nibss-digital-payment-fraud-drops-51-to-%E2%82%A625-85b-in-2025/; Punch, 2026, https://punchng.com/digital-payment-fraud-drops-from-n52-26bn-to-n25-85bn-in-2025-nibss/)
  • Gift-card-specific vectors: spoofed bank alerts/receipts, already-redeemed codes, DM-based off-platform deals; incumbents mitigate with BVN+ID+selfie KYC and settlement delays. (Source: WeeTracker, Sep 2025, https://weetracker.com/2025/09/09/inside-nigerias-usd-2-b-gift-card-economy-from-liquidity-fix-to-fintech-feature/; Owodex, 2025, https://www.owodex.com/blog/secure-gift-card-trading-in-nigeria-15-best-practices-to-avoid-scams-in-2025/)

4. Infrastructure & Vendor Landscape

4.1 Fiat rails (naira collection/payout)

  • Paystack / Flutterwave — dominant PSPs but both contractually exclude crypto businesses (see §2.10e); usable only for non-crypto legs unless policies change post-CBN 2026 pilot. (Source: Paystack, https://support.paystack.com/en/articles/2127042; Flutterwave, https://flutterwave.com/ng/support/my-account/businesses-and-services-prohibited-by-flutterwave)
  • Monnify (Moniepoint) — virtual accounts + bills API used by fintechs/neobanks. (Source: Monnify, https://monnify.com/products/bills-payment)
  • Providus Bank — banking APIs: transfers, dynamic virtual accounts, real-time webhooks; historically shut down fintech virtual accounts within days of the Feb 2021 CBN crypto circular — counterparty policy risk to price in. (Source: ProvidusBank, https://www.providusbank.com/banking-apis; Technext, Feb 8 2021, https://technext24.com/2021/02/08/why-providus-bank-shut-down-virtual-accounts-and-how-it-affects-fintechs-in-nigeria/)
  • 9PSB (payment service bank) — virtual account infrastructure for businesses. (Source: TechCabal, May 22 2025, https://techcabal.com/2025/05/22/virtual-accounts-for-businesses-9psb-is-enhancing-customer-experience-helping-businesses-scale/)

4.2 Bill payment aggregators

  • VTpass — airtime/data (MTN, Airtel, Glo, 9mobile), DSTV/GOTV/Startimes, electricity discos; documented public API incl. electricity payment API. (Source: VTpass docs, https://www.vtpass.com/documentation/electricity-payment-api/)
  • Interswitch Quickteller — 7,000+ billers, instant value, VAS APIs. (Source: Interswitch docs, https://docs.interswitchgroup.com/docs/value-added-services-overview)
  • Baxi (Onafriq) — 248 billers, 460,000+ agent network. (Source: Onafriq, https://onafriq.com/insights/article/selecting-the-right-bill-payment-api-in-nigeria)

4.3 Liquidity / OTC

  • Yellow Card — largest licensed stablecoin infrastructure provider in Africa (34 countries, 20 African); OTC desk for institutional volume; Payments API with local fiat on/off-ramps and treasury liquidity; first VASP licensed on the continent; on the Fireblocks network. (Source: Yellow Card, https://yellowcard.io/api-suite; Fireblocks directory, https://www.fireblocks.com/network/yellowcard)
  • Busha / Quidax — licensed local exchanges; Quidax listed cNGN first; both potential liquidity partners or competitors. (Source: Techpoint, 2025, https://techpoint.africa/insight/techpoint-digest-1053/)
  • Global venue APIs (Binance API, etc.) remain the deepest liquidity source but carry Nigeria-specific political risk given the Binance dispute (§2.9).

4.4 Custody / wallet infrastructure

  • Fireblocks — MPC wallets-as-a-service, direct custody, payments blueprint for PSP user accounts; industry default for exchange treasury. (Source: Fireblocks, https://www.fireblocks.com/platforms/direct-custody-wallets-as-a-service/)
  • Blockradar (Nigerian, YC-adjacent profile) — stablecoin wallet infrastructure for emerging-market fintechs: programmable non-custodial wallets with built-in AML, treasury and developer API; crossed $300M transaction volume with ~100,000 wallets created in 2025; 100+ fintech customers across 8 countries; Circle Gateway day-one partner (Aug 2025); won Crypto Valley Startup Competition 2025. (Source: Disrupt Africa, Aug 18 2025, https://disruptafrica.com/2025/08/18/how-nigerias-blockradar-provides-stablecoin-wallet-infrastructure-for-emerging-market-fintechs/; BitKE, Dec 2025, https://bitcoinke.io/2025/12/blockradar-in-2025/)
  • BitGo — global qualified custody alternative (regulated custodian option for PoR/insurance narratives). SEC rules require custody via a registered Digital Assets Custodian — note Blockvault Custodian Ltd is in ARIP, a local licensed-custody option. (Source: Technext, Jul 3 2026, https://technext24.com/2026/07/03/sec-clears-luno-koinkoin-and-5-others/)

4.5 KYC / identity vendors

  • Smile ID — 9 ID authorities, 132M+ Nigerian ID records; NIN, BVN, phone, voter's ID; direct government-database BVN verification; biometric/selfie liveness. (Source: Smile ID, https://usesmileid.com/countries/nigeria/)
  • Dojah — single API for KYC/AML/BVN/NIN/CAC/document/biometric checks; volume-based pricing (database checks like BVN/NIN cheapest; biometric/AML dearer). (Source: Dojah, https://dojah.io/blog/kyc-pricing-nigeria)
  • Prembly (Identitypass) — API + no-code verification, background checks, multi-country African coverage. Youverify / VerifyMe — alternates commonly used for CBN-grade KYC. (Source: Dojah comparison, 2026, https://dojah.io/blog/best-identity-verification-api-africa-2026; AllBusiness Africa, https://allbusiness.africa/insights/build-kyc-infrastructure-smileid-youverify)

4.6 Market data

  • CoinGecko / Binance APIs are the standard free/cheap price-feed sources; no Nigeria-specific licensing issue in consuming market data, but display of naira pairs should use your own executable book, not the (now politically sensitive) informal P2P rate.

4.7 cNGN status

  • cNGN launched February 3, 2025 — Africa's first regulated (SEC-approved) naira stablecoin, issued via the Africa Stablecoin Consortium/WrappedCBDC, 1:1 commercial-bank reserves, multi-chain, KYC/AML-integrated. (Source: Ledger Insights, Feb 2025, https://www.ledgerinsights.com/nigerias-regulated-stablecoin-cngn-launches/; Businessday, 2025, https://businessday.ng/opinion/article/cngn-nigerias-first-regulated-stablecoin-explained/)
  • Adoption is thin: listed on Busha and Quidax; Yellow Card and Roqqu approached but uncommitted. Users still prefer USDT/USDC because a naira-pegged coin doesn't hedge naira depreciation. Growth reported at 77% month-on-month from a small base; Oct 2025 attestation showed ₦17.5M excess reserves above 1:1. Note: Wrapped CBDC Ltd holds SEC ARIP approval (Jul 2026 cohort). (Source: Dabafinance, 2025, https://dabafinance.com/en/news/nigeria-s-cngn-stablecoin-faces-adoption-hurdle-as-exchanges-hold-back; Condia, 2025, https://thecondia.com/trust-attestation-stablecoin-africa-cngn/)
  • Implication: list cNGN for regulator goodwill and instant naira settlement experiments, but build product economics around USDT/USDC.

5. Strategic Implications for Lentopay

5.1 Licensing path & realistic timeline (as of July 2026)

  1. Incorporate in Nigeria with physical office; resident CEO/MD; recruit ≥4 principal officers (incl. compliance officer) who can pass SEC fit-and-proper (Forms SEC 2/2D). (Source: SEC ARIP checklist, https://sec.gov.ng/about/resources/checklists/accelerated-regulatory-incubation-program-arip-checklist-for-vasp-onboarding/)
  2. Capitalize to the 2026 bar: plan for ₦2B (~$1.4M) minimum capital for a DAX (deadline for existing operators June 30, 2027 — a new entrant should simply meet it at application) + fidelity bond ≥25% + ₦2M ARIP processing fee + ₦30M registration fees + legal/advisory. Realistic all-in regulatory capital + costs: **₦2.1–2.5B ($1.5–1.8M)**. (Source: TechCabal, Jan 16 2026, https://techcabal.com/2026/01/16/sec-2-billion-minimum-capital-for-exchanges/; Goldsmiths LLP, https://www.goldsmithsllp.com/how-virtual-assets-service-providers-can-obtain-licenses-in-nigeria/)
  3. Apply into ARIP/FinPort via a registered solicitor → Approval-in-Principle (observed cadence: SEC batches approvals; 9–11 AIP holders as of July 2026, up from 2 in 2024) → operate under AIP ≤12 months → regularize to full VASP/DAX registration. Realistic timeline based on peer experience: 9–18 months to AIP, +12 months to full registration. (Source: Nairametrics, Jul 3 2026, http://nairametrics.com/2026/07/03/breaking-sec-grants-approval-in-principle-to-7-crypto-firms-under-regulatory-sandbox/; Mondaq VASP guide, 2025, https://www.mondaq.com/nigeria/fin-tech/1695342/how-virtual-assets-service-providers-vasps-can-obtain-licenses-in-nigeria)
  4. Parallel tracks: NDPC registration (data controller of major importance); AML program + NFIU reporting setup; bank negotiations for CBN-compliant VASP accounts (needs the SEC license); FIRS monthly-reporting pipeline (mandatory from Jan 2026); SCUML check with counsel on whether gift-card operations trigger separate DNFBP registration.
  5. Alternative fast path: partner with or acquire an existing AIP/licensed entity (Busha, Quidax, KoinKoin, Bitbarter, GIGX) — the SEC's slow, batched cadence makes "license by partnership" a genuine option for time-to-market.

5.2 Feature phasing (regulatory gating)

Feature Gate Phase
Buy/sell/swap + fiat ramp SEC AIP + bank VASP account Launch (core of license)
Bill payments Aggregator partnership (VTpass/Quickteller); no own PSSP needed Launch/Phase 1
Gift card trading AML/KYC hardening (BVN+ID+selfie), fraud ops team Phase 1–2
Savings/yield Full VASP registration + SEC product approval + licensed custodian + PoR Phase 2–3
E-Fund (unlisted/tokenized) DAOP/crowdfunding intermediary license (₦100M–₦1B capital) + per-offer SEC clearance Phase 3 / partner

5.3 Trust playbook (what the market punishes/rewards)

  • Publish proof-of-reserves and use a regulated custodian from day one (FTX/Patricia scars). Never commingle; never issue IOU tokens.
  • Market yields conservatively; avoid any fixed-return language (CBEX/ISA 2025 Ponzi provisions).
  • Over-invest in social-engineering defenses, device binding, and withdrawal cool-downs (NIBSS: social engineering is fraud vector #1).
  • Lean into compliance as brand: "SEC-licensed, FIRS-reporting, NDPC-registered" is a marketable differentiator against offshore apps in 2026.

Top 10 Takeaways for Lentopay

  1. The market is huge and stablecoin-shaped: $92.1B on-chain value into Nigeria (Jul 2024–Jun 2025), ~22M users, and USDT/USDC dominance driven by naira devaluation — build the product around stablecoins as savings/transfer instruments, with BTC/ETH as secondary. (Chainalysis 2025; Mular 2025)
  2. There is now exactly one legal way in: ISA 2025 makes digital assets securities; SEC ARIP → AIP → full VASP/DAX registration is the path, and the CBN's Dec 2023 guidelines mean no SEC license = no bank account. Budget ₦2B+ capital (Jan 2026 circular) and 9–18 months to AIP.
  3. The SEC is opening the gates in 2026: from 2 provisional licensees (2024) to ~11 AIP holders including Luno and KuCoin Nigeria (Jul 2026). Speed matters — apply now or partner with an AIP holder before global entrants saturate the licensed tier.
  4. Tax compliance is a launch-blocking system requirement: from Jan 1, 2026, exchanges must file monthly per-user transaction reports to FIRS, and user gains are taxable (10% CGT baseline, up to 25% as income — verify final gazetted rates). Build the reporting pipeline into the ledger from day one.
  5. The Binance saga defines the downside: $81.5B lawsuit, executive detention, forced P2P naira delisting. Operate onshore, licensed, with resident leadership and proactive regulator engagement — and expect data-sharing demands.
  6. Each PRD feature has a different regulator and gate: trading (SEC VASP), bill payments (partner with licensed aggregators — VTpass/Quickteller/Baxi — to avoid CBN PSSP licensing), gift cards (AML-heavy, no dedicated license), savings/yield (SEC product approval + licensed custodian), E-Fund (DAOP/crowdfunding rules, per-offer pre-clearance — heaviest, phase last).
  7. Mainstream PSPs won't bank you for cards: Paystack and Flutterwave contractually exclude crypto businesses; plan on virtual-account bank-transfer rails (Providus, 9PSB, Monnify) negotiated bilaterally once licensed — and watch the CBN's Mar 2026 VASP-supervision pilot for thaw.
  8. Trust is the scarcest asset: Patricia ($2M hack → IOU token debacle), FTX contagion, and CBEX (200k+ victims, Apr 2025) have made Nigerians deeply skeptical. Proof-of-reserves, regulated custody (Fireblocks/BitGo/Blockvault), instant breach disclosure and conservative yield marketing are competitive weapons, not costs.
  9. Fraud ops is a core product function: social engineering is Nigeria's #1 fraud vector (NIBSS 2025: ₦25.85B losses); gift cards are a known scam-proceeds channel; CBN now debits receiving institutions for fraud proceeds. BVN+NIN+selfie KYC (Smile ID/Dojah), settlement delays, and device intelligence are table stakes.
  10. Ride the rails that exist: Yellow Card (liquidity/OTC), Blockradar (stablecoin wallet infra, $300M volume 2025), Smile ID/Dojah (KYC), VTpass/Interswitch (bills), cNGN (list for goodwill, don't depend on it). Nigeria-first infrastructure is mature enough that Lentopay should integrate, not build, everything except its core exchange and compliance stack.
